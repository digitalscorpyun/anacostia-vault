## **Overview**

Colonel Charles Young (1864–1922) was a pioneering African American military officer, educator, and diplomat. As the third African American to graduate from the U.S. Military Academy at West Point, he overcame racial barriers to achieve the highest rank held by a Black officer in the U.S. Army during his time. Young served with distinction in the Buffalo Soldiers, led troops in combat, and played a vital role in the development of military strategies and infrastructure in the early 20th century.

---

## **Key Details**

- **Born:** March 12, 1864, Mays Lick, Kentucky, USA
- **Died:** January 8, 1922, Lagos, Nigeria
- **Occupation:** Military Officer, Diplomat, Educator
- **Education:** United States Military Academy at West Point (Graduated 1889)
- **Military Achievements:**
    - Served with the 9th and 10th Cavalry Regiments (Buffalo Soldiers)
    - First African American National Park Superintendent (Sequoia National Park)
    - Military Attaché to Haiti and Liberia
    - Leadership roles in the Philippine-American War and the Mexican Expedition

---

## **Timeline**

- **1889:** Graduates from West Point, becoming the third African American to do so.
- **1894:** Assigned to the 9th Cavalry, Buffalo Soldiers, stationed in the American West.
- **1903:** Becomes the first African American superintendent of a national park (Sequoia National Park), overseeing infrastructure projects.
- **1916:** Serves with distinction during the Mexican Expedition under General Pershing.
- **1917:** Promoted to Lieutenant Colonel but controversially retired due to alleged health concerns, widely seen as racial discrimination.
- **1920:** Rides 500 miles on horseback to Washington, D.C., to prove his fitness for duty.
- **1922:** Dies while serving as U.S. military attaché to Liberia; later buried with honors at Arlington National Cemetery.

## **Impact & Legacy**

Colonel Charles Young’s legacy is one of perseverance, leadership, and excellence in the face of systemic racism. He inspired future generations of African American military personnel and was posthumously promoted to Brigadier General in 2021, recognizing his contributions to the U.S. military. His work in infrastructure, education, and diplomacy showcased his commitment to uplifting African Americans and strengthening international ties with Africa.
