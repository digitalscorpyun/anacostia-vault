## 📜 **The 👑 Royal Purple Beacon – Portrait’s Legacy**

1. **Historical Context** 🟣  
   Created c. 1773 as the frontispiece for Wheatley’s London-published book, the portrait shows her at a desk, pen poised, in a pose echoing John Singleton Copley’s style—[[phillis-wheatley-writing-pose]]. This 👑 royal purple image breaks from Copley’s exclusion of writing women, challenging colonial norms.

2. **Artistic Attribution** 🟣  
   Scholars suggest Scipio Moorhead, enslaved by Reverend John Moorhead, may have drawn it, based on Wheatley’s poem “To S. M. a Young African Painter”—[[africana-studies-scipio-moorhead]]. Yet, the lack of 18th- or 19th-century confirmation raises questions about establishment bias in crediting Black artists—[[africana-studies-systemic-racism]].

3. **Cultural Significance** 🟣  
   As the first depiction of a Black woman writing, it defies stereotypes—[[africana-studies-educational-equity]]. The engraving, held by institutions like the National Portrait Gallery, symbolizes Wheatley’s literary triumph—[[the-lion-of-anacostia-bias-detection]].

4. **Loss and Mystery** 🟣  
   The original painting is lost, with only engravings surviving. This 🏰 royal purple silence critiques historical neglect of Black art—[[africana-studies-oral-traditions]].

5. **Modern Relevance** 🟣
