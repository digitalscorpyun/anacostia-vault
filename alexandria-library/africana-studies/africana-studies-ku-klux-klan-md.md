# Ku Klux Klan (KKK)

**Type:** Organization  
**Date Created:** 2025-01-26  
**Last Updated:** 2025-01-26  

## Overview

The **Ku Klux Klan (KKK)** is a white supremacist organization that was founded in the aftermath of the **American Civil War** in **1865**. It has been dedicated to promoting white supremacy, racial segregation, and the oppression of African Americans and other marginalized groups through intimidation, violence, and political influence. The Klan has resurged at various points in U.S. history, particularly during Reconstruction, the Civil Rights Movement, and in response to modern racial justice efforts.

---

## Key Details

- **Founded:** December 24, 1865  
- **Founders:** Confederate veterans, including **Nathan Bedford Forrest** (first Grand Wizard)  
- **Ideology:** White supremacy, racism, nativism, anti-immigration, antisemitism, anti-Catholicism  
- **Symbols:** White robes, burning crosses, hooded figures  
- **Motto:** "The Invisible Empire"  

---

## Timeline

### **1st Klan: Reconstruction Era (1865–1870s)**  
- **Purpose:**  
  - Founded in Pulaski, Tennessee, initially as a social club but quickly turned into a terrorist organization aimed at suppressing African American civil rights through violence and intimidation.
  - Targeted newly freed African Americans, white Reconstruction supporters (scalawags), and Northerners (carpetbaggers).
- **Key Actions:**  
  - Night raids involving lynchings, beatings, and property destruction to prevent African Americans from voting and exercising their rights.  
  - Congress responded with the **Enforcement Acts (1870-1871)**, also known as the **Ku Klux Klan Acts**, which temporarily weakened the organization.  
- **Decline:**  
  - Federal enforcement efforts led to the suppression of the Klan by the mid-1870s.  

---

### **2nd Klan: Early 20th Century Revival (1915–1940s)**  
- **Resurgence:**  
  - Sparked by the 1915 film _The Birth of a Nation_, which glorified the Klan's actions during Reconstruction.  
  - Grew in response to increasing immigration, the Great Migration, and the rise of the Civil Rights Movement.  
- **Expansion:**  
  - The Klan reached millions of members nationwide, expanding beyond the South into the Midwest and West.  
  - Influenced politics, enforcing segregationist policies and opposing labor unions and civil rights activists.  
- **Decline:**  
  - Internal corruption and exposure by journalists led to declining membership by the 1940s.  

---

### **3rd Klan: Civil Rights Era (1950s–1970s)**  
- **Purpose:**  
  - Opposed desegregation, the Civil Rights Movement, and racial integration efforts.  
  - Committed acts of domestic terrorism, including the **bombing of the 16th Street Baptist Church** in Birmingham, Alabama (1963), killing four African American girls.  
- **Key Actions:**  
  - Targeted figures like **Martin Luther King Jr.**, civil rights workers, and activists during Freedom Rides and voter registration efforts.  
  - Faced opposition from the FBI under the COINTELPRO program.  
- **Decline:**  
  - Civil rights legislation, federal crackdowns, and public outcry weakened the Klan's influence by the late 1970s.  

---

### **4th Klan: Modern-Day Activity (1980s–Present)**  
- **Current Status:**  
  - While no longer a centralized organization, various Klan factions remain active, promoting hate through online platforms and localized actions.  
  - The Klan has aligned itself with broader white nationalist movements and participated in events like the **Charlottesville Unite the Right rally (2017)**.  
- **Challenges:**  
  - Increased scrutiny from the Southern Poverty Law Center (SPLC) and other anti-hate organizations.  
  - Internal divisions and competition from newer white supremacist groups such as the **Proud Boys** and **Oath Keepers**.  

---

## Notable Events and Crimes

- **1868–1870s:** Wave of racial violence targeting Black communities across the South.  
- **1915:** The release of _The Birth of a Nation_, which glorified the Klan and spurred recruitment.  
- **1920s:** March of 50,000 Klansmen in Washington, D.C., demonstrating national influence.  
- **1964:** Murders of civil rights workers James Chaney, Andrew Goodman, and Michael Schwerner in Mississippi (Freedom Summer).  
- **1981:** Lynching of Michael Donald in Alabama, leading to a landmark lawsuit against the Klan.

## Legacy and Impact

The Ku Klux Klan’s legacy of racial terror and violence continues to shape discussions on racism, white supremacy, and domestic terrorism in the United States. Despite legal measures and public condemnation, the Klan's influence persists in modern white nationalist and far-right movements.

**Key Impacts:**  
1. **Voter Suppression:** Efforts to prevent African Americans from voting through intimidation and violence, with long-lasting impacts on Black political participation.  
2. **Institutional Racism:** Helped entrench systemic racism through legal and extralegal means, influencing segregation policies and discriminatory laws.  
3. **Resistance:** Inspired generations of civil rights activists to fight for justice and equality in the face of terror.
