# Mary S. Peake

**Type:** Person  
**Date Created:** 2025-01-26  
**Last Updated:** 2025-01-26  

## Overview

**Mary Smith Peake** (1823 – February 22, 1862) was an African-American educator and abolitionist best known for her pioneering efforts to educate freed and enslaved African Americans in the South during the Civil War era. She is often credited with establishing the first formal school for African Americans under the Emancipation Oak in Hampton, Virginia, laying the groundwork for what would later become Hampton University.

---

## Key Details

- **Born:** 1823, Norfolk, Virginia  
- **Died:** February 22, 1862, Hampton, Virginia  
- **Occupation:** Educator, Abolitionist  
- **Known For:**  
  - Founding the first school for freed African Americans in Hampton, Virginia  
  - Advocating for literacy and education as tools for Black empowerment  
  - Teaching under the historic Emancipation Oak  

---

## Timeline

- **1823 (19th Century):** Born in Norfolk, Virginia to a free Black mother and a French father.  
- **1830s (19th Century):** Attended school in Alexandria, Virginia, despite laws restricting Black education.  
- **1851 (19th Century):** Married Thomas Peake, a formerly enslaved man.  
- **1861 (19th Century):** Began teaching formerly enslaved African Americans at Fort Monroe under the Emancipation Oak.  
- **1862 (19th Century):** Passed away from tuberculosis, but her legacy continued through the school she founded.

## Contributions to Education and Civil Rights

### **1. Early Efforts in Education**
- Despite legal restrictions on Black education, Peake taught African Americans in secret before the Civil War.
- Her commitment to literacy was grounded in the belief that education was essential to freedom and self-determination.

### **2. Emancipation Oak and the First School**
- In 1861, Peake established a school under the Emancipation Oak at Fort Monroe, Virginia, teaching over **50 students**, including adults and children.
- Her school became one of the first efforts to formally educate freed African Americans in the South during the war.

### **3. Support from the American Missionary Association**
- Peake's work was supported by the **American Missionary Association (AMA)**, which provided resources for her school and helped expand educational efforts for freed slaves.

---

## Challenges and Resilience

1. **Legal Restrictions:**  
   - Black education was heavily restricted under Virginia law, and Peake's work was considered illegal before the war.  
   - She had to teach in secret to avoid persecution.

2. **Health Challenges:**  
   - Peake battled tuberculosis while continuing to teach, demonstrating her unwavering commitment to the cause of education.

---

## Legacy and Impact

1. **Hampton University Connection:**  
   - Peake’s school laid the foundation for what would become **Hampton University**, a leading historically Black university.

2. **Symbol of African-American Education:**  
   - Her legacy continues to inspire generations of educators and activists who see literacy as a pathway to empowerment and liberation.

3. **Recognition:**  
   - The Emancipation Oak, under which she taught, is now a national landmark, symbolizing the quest for freedom and education.
