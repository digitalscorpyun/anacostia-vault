# Harriet Tubman

**Type:** Historical Figure  
**Date Created:** 2025-01-26

## Overview  

**Harriet Tubman** (c. 1822 – March 10, 1913) was an African-American abolitionist, humanitarian, and Union spy during the American Civil War. Known as the "Moses of her people," Tubman was instrumental in the Underground Railroad, helping enslaved individuals escape to freedom. Her legacy as a symbol of courage, resistance, and perseverance endures in American history and Africana Studies.  

---

## Key Details  

- **Born:** c. 1822, Dorchester County, Maryland  
- **Died:** March 10, 1913, Auburn, New York  
- **Known For:**  
  - Leading enslaved people to freedom via the [[Africana Studies - Underground Railroad]].  
  - Serving as a spy, nurse, and scout for the Union Army during the Civil War.  
  - Advocating for women's suffrage and racial equality after the war.

## Timeline  

- **1822 (19th Century):** Born into slavery in Maryland.  
- **1849 (19th Century):** Escaped slavery and began working with the Underground Railroad.  
- **1850s (19th Century):** Conducted numerous missions to free enslaved individuals.  
- **1861–1865 (19th Century):** Served as a spy and scout for the Union Army during the Civil War.  
- **1863 (19th Century):** Led the successful Combahee River Raid, freeing over 700 enslaved people.  
- **1869 (19th Century):** Married Nelson Davis, a Union Army veteran.  
- **1913 (20th Century):** Passed away in Auburn, New York, at approximately 91 years old.

## Contributions and Achievements  

### **1. Underground Railroad**  
- Tubman personally led approximately **13 missions**, freeing around **70 enslaved people**, and provided guidance to many others.  
- She worked with prominent abolitionists such as [[Frederick Douglass]] and [[William Still]].  
- Used secret networks and safe houses to guide freedom seekers to Northern states and Canada.  

### **2. Civil War Service**  
- Worked as a scout, nurse, and spy for the Union Army, gathering intelligence behind Confederate lines.  
- Led the **Combahee River Raid** in 1863, which liberated over 700 enslaved people in South Carolina.  
- Advocated for equal pay and better treatment for Black soldiers and laborers.  

### **3. Women's Suffrage and Activism**  
- After the war, Tubman became a strong advocate for **women’s suffrage**, working alongside suffragists like [[Susan B. Anthony]].  
- Spoke at national gatherings to promote the rights of African Americans and women.  
- Established the **Harriet Tubman Home for the Aged**, providing care for elderly African Americans.  

---

## Challenges and Struggles  

- Suffered a traumatic head injury as a child from a blow by an overseer, which caused lifelong seizures and visions.  
- Despite her contributions, Tubman struggled with poverty and was often denied recognition and financial support.  
- Endured numerous threats to her life while rescuing enslaved people, demonstrating incredible bravery and resilience.  

---

## Legacy and Honors  

1. **Cultural Impact:**  
   - Tubman has become a symbol of resistance and empowerment in African-American history.  
   - Her life and legacy are celebrated in books, films, and educational curricula.  

2. **National Recognition:**  
   - In 2016, the U.S. Treasury Department announced plans to place Tubman’s image on the **$20 bill**, recognizing her enduring legacy.  
   - Numerous schools, parks, and institutions are named in her honor.  

3. **Representation in Media:**  
   - Films such as _Harriet_ (2019) have brought Tubman’s story to new generations.  
   - She is frequently referenced in literature, music, and political discourse as a figure of inspiration.  

---

## Africana Studies Relation  

Harriet Tubman’s life and work are essential to Africana Studies, providing critical insights into resistance, agency, and the fight for freedom. Her efforts in the Underground Railroad, military service, and advocacy highlight the intersections of race, gender, and social justice in African-American history.
