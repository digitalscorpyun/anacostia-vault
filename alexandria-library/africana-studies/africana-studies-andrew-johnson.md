# Andrew Johnson

**Type:** Person  
**Date Created:** 2025-01-26  
**Last Updated:** 2025-01-26  

## Overview

**Andrew Johnson** (December 29, 1808 – July 31, 1875) was the 17th president of the United States, serving from **1865 to 1869** following the assassination of **[[Abraham Lincoln]]**. Johnson, a Southern Democrat who remained loyal to the Union during the Civil War, is widely known for his controversial policies during the [[Africana Studies - Reconstruction Era]] , his leniency toward former Confederate states, and his impeachment by the U.S. House of Representatives.

---

## Key Details

- **Born:** December 29, 1808, in Raleigh, North Carolina  
- **Died:** July 31, 1875, in Elizabethton, Tennessee  
- **Political Party:** Democrat (later aligned with the National Union Party during Lincoln’s second term)  
- **Vice President:** None (He ascended to the presidency after Lincoln’s death)  
- **Preceded by:** Abraham Lincoln  
- **Succeeded by:** Ulysses S. Grant  
- **Key Policies:** Opposition to Radical Reconstruction, leniency toward Southern states, opposition to civil rights protections for freed African Americans  

---

## Timeline

- **1862 (19th Century):** Appointed military governor of Tennessee by President Lincoln.  
- **1864 (19th Century):** Elected Vice President under Abraham Lincoln's National Union ticket.  
- **1865 (19th Century):** Became president following Lincoln's assassination.  
- **1866 (19th Century):** Vetoed the [[Civil Rights Act of 1866]], which Congress later overrode.  
- **1867 (19th Century):** Impeached by the U.S. House of Representatives but acquitted by the Senate.  
- **1875 (19th Century):** Elected to the U.S. Senate but died later that year.

## Presidency and Reconstruction

Johnson's presidency is primarily marked by his approach to **Reconstruction**, which sought to quickly restore Southern states to the Union with minimal federal oversight. His policies included:

1. **Leniency Towards the South:**  
   - Johnson issued widespread pardons to former Confederates.  
   - Allowed Southern states to re-enter the Union without strict requirements on civil rights for freedmen.  

2. **Veto of Civil Rights Legislation:**  
   - Opposed the **[[Civil Rights Act of 1866]]**, which aimed to grant equal legal protections to African Americans.  
   - Opposed the **[[Reconstruction Acts]]**, which imposed military rule in the South and required states to adopt new constitutions guaranteeing Black rights.  

3. **Opposition to the 14th Amendment:**  
   - Johnson vehemently opposed granting citizenship and equal protection under the law to formerly enslaved individuals.  

4. **Clash with Congress:**  
   - His resistance to Congressional Reconstruction led to his impeachment in 1868, making him the first U.S. president to face impeachment.  
   - He narrowly avoided removal from office by one vote in the Senate.  

---

## Impeachment

- Johnson was impeached by the House of Representatives primarily due to his violation of the **Tenure of Office Act**, which restricted his ability to remove federal officials without Senate approval.  
- He attempted to remove Secretary of War **Edwin Stanton**, leading to his impeachment.  
- The Senate trial resulted in his acquittal by just one vote.  

---

## Legacy and Impact

Johnson's presidency is widely criticized for:

1. **Failure to Protect Freedmen:**  
   - His opposition to radical Reconstruction policies left African Americans vulnerable to oppression through **[[Black Codes]]** and systemic racism.  
   
2. **Stalling Reconstruction Progress:**  
   - By allowing Southern states to reinstate former Confederate leaders and policies, Johnson contributed to the persistence of racial discrimination.  

3. **Constitutional Precedent:**  
   - His impeachment set a historical precedent, influencing later cases of presidential accountability.  

Despite his controversial presidency, Johnson's advocacy for limited federal government and states' rights left a lasting impact on American politics.

## Africana Studies Relation

Andrew Johnson’s presidency had a profound impact on African Americans, particularly in the Reconstruction era. His lenient policies toward the South and opposition to civil rights legislation significantly hindered Black progress and contributed to systemic racial disparities that persisted well into the 20th century.
