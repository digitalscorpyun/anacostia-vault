### **Structured Analysis: "How DEI Built America" by Michael Harriot**  

---

#### **1️⃣ Key Themes**  
- **White Supremacy as Proto-DEI**: Systemic policies favoring whiteness (immigration quotas, voter suppression) functioned as America’s original "Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion" (DEI) programs, designed to maintain racial hierarchy.  
- **Pseudoscientific Racism**: Enlightenment-era theories (Buffon’s environmental determinism, Jefferson’s genetic superiority) laid groundwork for codified exclusion.  
- **Racial Capitalism**: Policies like the Johnson-Reed Act (1924) preserved economic and political power for whites, mirroring Cedric Robinson’s analysis of race and capitalism.  

---

#### **2️⃣ Historical Context**  
- **1749**: French naturalist **Georges-Louis Leclerc (Buffon)** posits whiteness as humanity’s "original stock," framing non-Europeans as "degenerate."  
- **1781**: **Thomas Jefferson**’s *Notes on the State of Virginia* asserts Black genetic inferiority to justify slavery and propose expulsion of freed Blacks, fearing racial "mixture."  
- **1898**: **Louisiana Constitution** enacts grandfather clauses and literacy tests to disenfranchise Black voters while protecting white suffrage.  
- **1924**: **Johnson-Reed Act** imposes immigration quotas based on the 1920 census (peak U.S. whiteness) to restrict non-European migration.  

---

#### **3️⃣ Philosophical Insights**  
- **Epistemic Violence**: Buffon and Jefferson weaponized "science" to naturalize racial hierarchies, a practice critiqued by **Sylvia Wynter** (*The Ceremony Must Be Found*).  
- **Moral Contradictions**: Jefferson’s bill to emancipate slaves while banishing them reflects the hypocrisy of "freedom" in a settler-colonial state, akin to **Patrick Wolfe**’s analysis of eliminationist policies.  
- **Structural Racism**: Harriot echoes **Cedric Robinson**’s *Black Marxism*, showing how racial capitalism relies on codified exclusion.  

---

#### **4️⃣ Connections to Africana Thought**  
- **W.E.B. Du Bois** (*Black Reconstruction*): The Louisiana grandfather clause exemplifies Du Bois’ "wages of whiteness," where poor whites gained symbolic status over Blacks.  
- **Ibram X. Kendi** (*Stamped from the Beginning*): Jefferson’s racial theories align with Kendi’s framework of racist ideas as tools of power.  
- **Michelle Alexander** (*The New Jim Crow*): The 1924 immigration law mirrors modern carceral systems that target marginalized groups.  

---

#### **5️⃣ Practical Applications**  
- **Policy Analysis**: Audit "neutral" laws (zoning, education funding) for historical racial biases using Harriot’s framework.  
- **Education**: Integrate primary sources (e.g., Jefferson’s *Notes*) into curricula to expose foundational racism.  
- **Advocacy**: Counter anti-DEI backlash by highlighting how "colorblind" policies often replicate historical exclusion.  

---

#### **6️⃣ Suggested Further Reading**  
- *Notes on the State of Virginia* by Thomas Jefferson (1785) – Primary source for Jefferson’s racial theories.  
- *The Condemnation of Blackness* by Khalil Gibran Muhammad – Traces pseudoscientific racism in policy.  
- *Impossible Subjects* by Mae Ngai – Examines the Johnson-Reed Act’s racial quotas.  
- *Stamped from the Beginning* (Graphic Novel) – Accessible critique of racist ideas.  

---

**Final Reflection**:  
Harriot’s essay challenges us to see "DEI" not as a modern innovation but as a historical practice of privileging whiteness. By reframing white supremacy as America’s original diversity program, he forces a confrontation with the nation’s foundational inequities—a necessary step toward transformative justice. As **James Baldwin** wrote, *"Not everything that is faced can be changed, but nothing can be changed until it is faced."*
