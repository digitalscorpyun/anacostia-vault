# 333rd Field Artillery Battalion

**Type:** Military Unit  
**Date Created:** 2025-01-26  
**Last Updated:** 2025-01-26  

## Overview
The **333rd Field Artillery Battalion** was a segregated African-American unit in the United States Army during **World War II**. Known for their service and sacrifice, the battalion played a significant role in the European Theater, particularly during the **Battle of the Bulge**.

---

## Key Details
- **Activated:** August 5, 1942, at **Camp Gruber, Oklahoma**.  
- **Composition:** African-American enlisted soldiers commanded by white officers, following segregationist policies.  
- **Equipment:** Armed with **155mm howitzers**, providing artillery support to infantry units.  

---

## Timeline
- **August 5, 1942 (20th Century):** Battalion activated at Camp Gruber, Oklahoma.  
- **July 1944 (20th Century):** Landed at Utah Beach, Normandy.  
- **December 17, 1944 (20th Century):** Wereth 11 tragedy occurs.  
- **1948 (20th Century):** Desegregation of the U.S. Armed Forces, partly influenced by units like the 333rd.

## World War II Service

1. **Normandy and Northern France:**  
    - Landed at **Utah Beach** in **July 1944**, providing artillery support during the **siege of Brest** and northern France campaigns.  

2. **Battle of the Bulge (December 1944):**  
    - Stationed in **Schoenberg, Belgium**, supporting the **U.S. VIII Corps Artillery**.  
    - Overwhelmed by the German Ardennes Offensive; elements of the unit, including **Service Battery** and **C Battery**, remained behind, suffering heavy casualties.  

---

## The Wereth 11

A tragic and little-known event associated with the 333rd Field Artillery Battalion:

- **The Incident:** Eleven African-American soldiers, known as the **[[Wereth 11]]**, were captured after escaping advancing German forces.  
- **The Atrocity:** Betrayed by locals in **Wereth, Belgium**, captured by **SS troops**, tortured, and executed on **December 17, 1944**.  
- **Recognition:**  
    - Their story remained largely untold for decades.  
    - A memorial in Wereth now honors their sacrifice and highlights the systemic racism they faced while serving their country.  

---

## Impact & Legacy

1. **Recognition:**  
    - The 333rd Field Artillery Battalion demonstrated the bravery of African-American soldiers despite segregation and discrimination.  
    - Memorialized in documentaries, books, and historical accounts.  

2. **Memorials:**  
    - The **Wereth 11 Memorial** in Belgium honors the executed soldiers.  
    - Recognized at the **National Museum of African American History and Culture** and the **National WWII Museum**.  

3. **Impact:**  
    - The sacrifices of the 333rd Battalion contributed to President Truman's decision to desegregate the U.S. Armed Forces in **1948**.  

---

## Significance

The story of the **333rd Field Artillery Battalion** highlights both the heroism and the systemic discrimination faced by African-American soldiers during World War II. Their service stands as a testament to resilience, sacrifice, and the fight for equality.
