## **Overview**

Emmitt J. Scott (1873–1957) was a prominent African American journalist, author, and public official. He is best known for serving as the personal secretary to Booker T. Washington at the Tuskegee Institute and later becoming a special adviser to President Woodrow Wilson on African American affairs during World War I. Scott played a crucial role in advocating for African American soldiers and promoting their contributions to the war effort.

---

## **Key Details**

- **Born:** February 13, 1873, Houston, Texas, USA
- **Died:** December 12, 1957
- **Occupation:** Journalist, Author, Government Official
- **Education:** Wiley College (Historically Black College)
- **Key Roles:**
    - Secretary to Booker T. Washington (1897–1915)
    - Special Assistant for Negro Affairs, U.S. Department of War (1917–1919)
    - Editor of _The Colored American Magazine_
    - Business Manager at Tuskegee Institute

## **Impact & Legacy**

Emmitt Scott's contributions to African American education, journalism, and government service paved the way for greater inclusion of Black Americans in public affairs. His role during World War I was instrumental in addressing racial discrimination within the military and ensuring that African American soldiers were recognized for their contributions. His legacy lives on through his writings and efforts in improving the social standing of African Americans in the early 20th century.

## **Africana Studies Relation**

Scott's work intersects with Africana studies through his commitment to racial uplift, advocacy for African American soldiers, and efforts to document Black achievements in history. His close association with Booker T. Washington also highlights key themes of self-help and racial progress in the post-Reconstruction era.
