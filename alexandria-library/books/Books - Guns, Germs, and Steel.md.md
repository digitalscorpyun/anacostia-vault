# 🌍 Guns, Germs, and Steel  
*Why did some societies dominate while others lagged?*  

## 📋 Overview  
Jared Diamond’s *Guns, Germs, and Steel* unpacks how geography, resources, and timing—not human superiority—shaped global inequality. From Yali’s question in New Guinea to today’s linguistic losses, it’s a lens on history’s uneven trajectories.  

### 🔑 Key Points  
- **Geography Wins**: Fertile land (e.g., Eurasia’s wheat) + domesticable animals (horses, cows) = food surplus → tech → power. Africa’s zebras and lions? Not so tame.  
- **Germs as Weapons**: Eurasian diseases (smallpox) wiped out natives elsewhere—90% of Americas’ population gone post-Columbus.  
- **Linguistic Fallout**: 6,000 languages today, half dying soon. English, Mandarin, Russian rise with historical "cargo."  

## 🌟 Connections to My Interests  
- **Africana Studies** ✊🏿: Africa’s diversity—800+ languages in Papua New Guinea alone—got outmaneuvered by Eurasia’s head start. Swahili thrives; smaller tongues fade.  
- **Social Justice** ⚖️: Cultural erasure isn’t random—it’s the colonized losing ground. Fairness demands preserving these voices.  
- **AI & Python** 🤖: Could AI map Diamond’s variables to modern gaps? Scrape Ethnologue with BeautifulSoup, analyze with Pandas, predict with TensorFlow.
