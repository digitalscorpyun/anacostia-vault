### Introduction
- <span style="color: purple;">We write when we need to remember something, be it an idea, a quote or the outcome of a study.</span>
- We write when we want to organize our thoughts and when we want to exchange ideas with others. 
- We write down not only those things we fear we won’t remember otherwise, but also the very things we try to memorize.
- Everyday note-taking takes up the biggest chunk of writing.
- Turn your thoughts and discoveries into convincing written pieces and build up a treasure of smart and interconnected notes along the way.
- <span style="color: purple;">Every intellectual endeavor begins with note.</span>
- But most of all, you can write every day in a way that brings your projects forward.
- What does make a significant difference along the whole intelligence spectrum is something else: how much **self-discipline** or **self-control** one uses to approach the tasks at hand (Duckworth and Seligman, 2005; Tangney, Baumeister, and Boone, 2004).
- <span style="color: purple;">Having a meaningful and well-defined task beats willpower every time.</span>
- A good structure is something you can trust.
- Skills to detect and think through dis-confirming facts and arguments ([[Critical Thinking]], metacognition, logical fallacies).
### 1.3 The slip-box manual
	- Strictly speaking, Luhmann had two slip-boxes: a bibliographical one, which contained the references and brief notes on the content of the literature, and the main one in which he collected and generated his ideas, mainly in response to what he read.
	- The notes were written on index cards and stored in wooden boxes.
	- Bibliographic information on one side of a card and brief notes about the content on the other side. These notes would end up in the **bibliographic slip-box**.
	- In a second step, shortly after, he would look at his brief notes and think about their relevance for his own thinking and writing.
	- He then would turn to the main slip-box and write his ideas, comments and thoughts on new pieces of paper, using only one for each idea and restricting himself to one side of the paper, to make it easier to read them later without having to take them out of the box.
	- He usually wrote his notes with an eye towards already existing notes in the slip-box.
	- By adding these links between notes, Luhmann was able to add the same note to different contexts.
	- Notes with a sorted collection of links are, of course, good entry points.
