# Maryland Senate Bill 587 (2025): Reparations in a Decolonial Afrofuturist Frame

## Overview of Maryland’s Reparations Bill

([Maryland Reparations Bill Head To Gov. Wes Moore For Approval](https://www.blackenterprise.com/reparations-bill-maryland/)) _Graffiti in Washington, D.C. demanding reparations – a sign of growing calls to confront historical injustices._  
Maryland’s Senate Bill 587 (2025) marks a landmark effort to address the enduring harms of slavery and institutional racism. The Legislative Black Caucus of Maryland championed this bill, which passed the House of Delegates by 101–36 and now heads to Governor Wes Moore’s desk ([Maryland Reparations Bill Head To Gov. Wes Moore For Approval](https://www.blackenterprise.com/reparations-bill-maryland/#:~:text=The%20bill%20passed%20the%20Maryland,been%20affected%20by%20inequitable%20policies)) ([House rejects GOP amendments, gives final approval to bill creating Reparations Commission - Maryland Matters](https://marylandmatters.org/2025/04/03/house-rejects-gop-amendments-gives-final-approval-to-bill-creating-reparations-commission/#:~:text=The%20House%20of%20Delegates%20gave,the%20governor%20for%20his%20signature)). If signed into law, SB 587 would establish a **Maryland Reparations Commission** empowered to study and recommend “appropriate benefits to be made” to those whose ancestors were enslaved in Maryland or who were harmed by **inequitable government policies** ([Lawmakers pass bill to study reparations for ancestors of Maryland slavery](https://www.wmar2news.com/infocus/maryland-reparations-commission-bill-sent-to-gov-moores-desk#:~:text=The%20commission%20would%20be%20unpaid,representatives%2C%20and%20state%20agency%20heads)) ([Lawmakers pass bill to study reparations for ancestors of Maryland slavery](https://www.wmar2news.com/infocus/maryland-reparations-commission-bill-sent-to-gov-moores-desk#:~:text=Some%20of%20the%20reparations%20to,debt%20forgiveness%2C%20and%20college%20tuition)). Eligible recipients are defined as individuals of African descent with Maryland enslaved ancestors or who have suffered from systemic discrimination in the state ([Maryland Reparations Bill Head To Gov. Wes Moore For Approval](https://www.blackenterprise.com/reparations-bill-maryland/#:~:text=The%20bill%20passed%20the%20Maryland,been%20affected%20by%20inequitable%20policies)). In other words, the reparations scope is deliberately broad, encompassing both the descendants of enslaved people and those directly impacted by Jim Crow-era policies of segregation and exclusion. This dual eligibility reflects an understanding that historical injustice in Maryland did not end with Emancipation, but continued through generations of discriminatory laws and practices.

SB 587 outlines a comprehensive suite of reparative measures aimed at redress in multiple domains – **monetary, social, housing, and educational**. Rather than a single payout, the legislation envisions reparations as a package of supports to uplift Black communities. Possible remedies include official **statements of apology** from the state, direct **monetary compensation**, **property tax rebates**, and expanded **social services assistance** ([Lawmakers pass bill to study reparations for ancestors of Maryland slavery](https://www.wmar2news.com/infocus/maryland-reparations-commission-bill-sent-to-gov-moores-desk#:~:text=Some%20of%20the%20reparations%20to,debt%20forgiveness%2C%20and%20college%20tuition)). The bill also proposes alleviating economic barriers by waiving or reimbursing certain fees (such as business licensing and permit fees) and providing help with **home down payments** for first-time buyers ([Maryland Reparations Bill Head To Gov. Wes Moore For Approval](https://www.blackenterprise.com/reparations-bill-maryland/#:~:text=The%20reparations%20package%20could%20include,tuition%20waivers%20for%20higher%20education)). Additional support under consideration features **small business incentives**, subsidized **childcare**, **debt forgiveness**, and **tuition waivers** for higher education ([Maryland Reparations Bill Head To Gov. Wes Moore For Approval](https://www.blackenterprise.com/reparations-bill-maryland/#:~:text=The%20reparations%20package%20could%20include,tuition%20waivers%20for%20higher%20education)). The inclusion of educational and housing aid shows an understanding that reparations must build generational wealth and opportunity, not merely issue checks. _“Reparations would come in multiple forms beyond financial payments,”_ explained Delegate Aletheia McCaskill, underscoring that the Commission will _“explore strategies to support and uplift vulnerable communities that continue to suffer the lingering and evasive effects of discriminatory practices rooted in systemic racism.”_ ([Maryland Reparations Bill Head To Gov. Wes Moore For Approval](https://www.blackenterprise.com/reparations-bill-maryland/#:~:text=Del,multiple%20forms%20beyond%20financial%20payments)) In short, SB 587’s vision of reparations is holistic: it seeks material compensation, but also structural change and healing for Black Marylanders.

## Historical Legacy of Slavery and Racism in Maryland

Any reparations initiative must be understood in light of the deep historical wounds it aims to heal. Maryland’s history of slavery and racial injustice is both long and uniquely impactful. As an early British colony, Maryland was a cradle of the American slave economy – enslaved Africans and their labor were foundational in building the state’s agriculture and infrastructure ([Maryland bill to address slavery and reparations awaits governor's decision](https://san.com/cc/maryland-bill-to-address-slavery-and-reparations-awaits-governors-decision/#:~:text=Many%20Maryland%20lawmakers%20want%20to,%E2%80%9D)). Maryland not only practiced slavery but also pioneered harsh **slave codes** that became models for other colonies and states ([Maryland bill to address slavery and reparations awaits governor's decision](https://san.com/cc/maryland-bill-to-address-slavery-and-reparations-awaits-governors-decision/#:~:text=Many%20Maryland%20lawmakers%20want%20to,%E2%80%9D)). These laws codified a racial caste system, stripping Black people of basic rights and controlling every aspect of enslaved life. Even after the Civil War and the abolition of slavery in 1865, the legacy persisted: during the post-Reconstruction and Jim Crow eras (1877–1965), Maryland systematically enforced segregation and discrimination that relegated Black citizens to second-class status ([Lawmakers pass bill to study reparations for ancestors of Maryland slavery](https://www.wmar2news.com/infocus/maryland-reparations-commission-bill-sent-to-gov-moores-desk#:~:text=individuals%20whose%20ancestors%20were%20enslaved,by%20certain%20inequitable%20government%20policies)). State and local policies mandated separate (and unequal) schools, restricted Black homeownership through redlining and restrictive covenants, and denied Black families access to many public services and economic opportunities ([Lawmakers pass bill to study reparations for ancestors of Maryland slavery](https://www.wmar2news.com/infocus/maryland-reparations-commission-bill-sent-to-gov-moores-desk#:~:text=individuals%20whose%20ancestors%20were%20enslaved,by%20certain%20inequitable%20government%20policies)). The bill itself explicitly acknowledges this **“history of slavery and racial discrimination in Maryland”**, noting that post-1865 policies in housing, education, and taxation _“led to economic disparities based on race.”_ ([Lawmakers pass bill to study reparations for ancestors of Maryland slavery](https://www.wmar2news.com/infocus/maryland-reparations-commission-bill-sent-to-gov-moores-desk#:~:text=individuals%20whose%20ancestors%20were%20enslaved,by%20certain%20inequitable%20government%20policies))

This legacy of exclusion had concrete, generational consequences. Black Marylanders paid taxes to a state that often denied them the corresponding benefits. As Delegate Stephanie Smith poignantly noted, _“There were roads they paid for they could not drive down. There were schools they paid for they could not enter. There were colleges they paid for they could not gain admission to.”_ ([Maryland bill to address slavery and reparations awaits governor's decision](https://san.com/cc/maryland-bill-to-address-slavery-and-reparations-awaits-governors-decision/#:~:text=Delegate%20Stephanie%20Smith%2C%20D,historical%20injustice%20to%20be%20addressed)) ([House rejects GOP amendments, gives final approval to bill creating Reparations Commission - Maryland Matters](https://marylandmatters.org/2025/04/03/house-rejects-gop-amendments-gives-final-approval-to-bill-creating-reparations-commission/#:~:text=But%20Del.%20Stephanie%20Smith%20%28D,receive%20the%20benefits%20from%20them)) Segregation meant that African American taxpayers literally funded public facilities and institutions from which they were legally barred. Such injustices in the late 19th and early 20th centuries exacerbated the racial wealth gap: Black families were prevented from accumulating property or accessing quality education, advantages that white families leveraged to build intergenerational wealth. Maryland also had incidents of racial terror, including lynchings and mob violence, that devastated Black communities and enforced white supremacy well into the 20th century. In short, the **“deep scars of racial injustice”** are embedded in Maryland’s social and economic foundations ([Black Caucus Breakdown with Del. Joseline A. Peña-Melnyk on SB ...](https://www.instagram.com/blackcaucusmd/reel/DH_5rxURbAZ/#:~:text=Black%20Caucus%20Breakdown%20with%20Del,a%20vital%20step%20toward)). This historical context provides the moral impetus for SB 587. The bill’s proponents argue that the profound harms inflicted by slavery and Jim Crow in Maryland require purposeful remediation today. By directly addressing the **“long-term effects of racial discrimination within the state,”** the proposed commission seeks to formally reckon with Maryland’s past in order to chart a more equitable future ([Maryland Reparations Bill Head To Gov. Wes Moore For Approval](https://www.blackenterprise.com/reparations-bill-maryland/#:~:text=SB%20587%20was%20filed%20alongside,effort%20to%20address%20systemic%20injustice)).

## The Maryland Reparations Commission: Provisions and Purpose

Under SB 587, Maryland would establish a **statewide Reparations Commission** to investigate historical wrongs and develop reparative solutions. This Commission, as proposed, will be a diverse body of unpaid members, including state lawmakers, representatives from Maryland’s Historically Black Colleges and Universities (HBCUs), and heads of key state agencies ([Lawmakers pass bill to study reparations for ancestors of Maryland slavery](https://www.wmar2news.com/infocus/maryland-reparations-commission-bill-sent-to-gov-moores-desk#:~:text=The%20commission%20would%20be%20unpaid,representatives%2C%20and%20state%20agency%20heads)). By involving HBCU scholars and public officials, the Commission will draw on both academic expertise in African American history and the policy tools of government. The Commission’s mandate is to conduct a thorough study of Maryland’s history of racial injustice – assessing specific federal, state, and local policies from 1877 to 1965 that created racial inequities – and to recommend tangible remedies ([House rejects GOP amendments, gives final approval to bill creating Reparations Commission - Maryland Matters](https://marylandmatters.org/2025/04/03/house-rejects-gop-amendments-gives-final-approval-to-bill-creating-reparations-commission/#:~:text=If%20approved%2C%20the%20commission%20would,policies%2C%E2%80%9D%20according%20to%20the%20bill)). Crucially, the Commission will examine not only government actions but also how **private institutions profited** from slavery and segregation, potentially identifying private entities that could contribute to reparations funding ([Lawmakers pass bill to study reparations for ancestors of Maryland slavery](https://www.wmar2news.com/infocus/maryland-reparations-commission-bill-sent-to-gov-moores-desk#:~:text=licensing%20and%20permit%20fee%20waivers%2C,debt%20forgiveness%2C%20and%20college%20tuition)). Indeed, the bill suggests that businesses and organizations in Maryland which historically benefited from enslaved labor or discriminatory laws should be considered as _“potential funding sources”_ for reparative initiatives ([Lawmakers pass bill to study reparations for ancestors of Maryland slavery](https://www.wmar2news.com/infocus/maryland-reparations-commission-bill-sent-to-gov-moores-desk#:~:text=While%20the%20bill%20doesn%27t%20specify,as%20potential%20funding%20sources)). This signals a broad, societal approach to reparations: responsibility is not placed solely on taxpayers, but also on institutions that amassed wealth through racial exploitation.

The **Legislative Black Caucus of Maryland** played a pivotal role in crafting and advancing this bill. For the first time in the state’s history, the Black Caucus made reparations a top legislative priority, signaling unified political will among Black lawmakers ([House rejects GOP amendments, gives final approval to bill creating Reparations Commission - Maryland Matters](https://marylandmatters.org/2025/04/03/house-rejects-gop-amendments-gives-final-approval-to-bill-creating-reparations-commission/#:~:text=It%20also%20helped%20that%2C%20for,the%20full%20chamber%20March%2014)). Senators C. Anthony Muse, Malcolm Augustine, Benjamin Brooks, and Mary Washington sponsored SB 587 in the Senate (with Delegate Aletheia McCaskill leading the House counterpart, HB 1422) ([Maryland Reparations Bill Head To Gov. Wes Moore For Approval](https://www.blackenterprise.com/reparations-bill-maryland/#:~:text=and%20help%20with%20housing%20down,tuition%20waivers%20for%20higher%20education)). The Caucus held public hearings and town halls, elevating testimonies from descendants of enslaved persons and elders who survived Jim Crow segregation. These personal stories of pain and perseverance underscored the urgency of action. _“This bill is needed because of the history of slavery in Maryland,”_ one advocate stated plainly ([House grants preliminary approval to reparations study measure](https://marylandmatters.org/2025/04/02/house-grants-preliminary-approval-to-reparations-study-measure/#:~:text=measure%20marylandmatters,of%20slavery%20in%20Maryland)). By framing reparations as a response to documented historical injustice rather than a handout, supporters built moral and political momentum. Their efforts paid off: after years of failed attempts, the cross-filed bill finally gained majority support in 2025, aided by strategic cross-filing in both chambers and the Black Caucus’s commitment ([House rejects GOP amendments, gives final approval to bill creating Reparations Commission - Maryland Matters](https://marylandmatters.org/2025/04/03/house-rejects-gop-amendments-gives-final-approval-to-bill-creating-reparations-commission/#:~:text=%E2%80%9CIt%E2%80%99s%20about%20ushering%20the%20purpose,%E2%80%9D)). SB 587’s passage makes Maryland only the **fourth state** in the nation to officially pursue a slavery reparations study, following precedents set by California (2020), Illinois (2021), and New York (2023) ([House rejects GOP amendments, gives final approval to bill creating Reparations Commission - Maryland Matters](https://marylandmatters.org/2025/04/03/house-rejects-gop-amendments-gives-final-approval-to-bill-creating-reparations-commission/#:~:text=The%20101,and%20New%20York%20in%202023)).

The Commission is expected to begin work in mid-2025 and operate through 2027, reflecting a rigorous, data-driven approach emblematic of **algorithmic justice** principles. It must deliver a preliminary report by January 1, 2027 and a final comprehensive report by November 1, 2027, detailing recommended reparations programs ([Lawmakers pass bill to study reparations for ancestors of Maryland slavery](https://www.wmar2news.com/infocus/maryland-reparations-commission-bill-sent-to-gov-moores-desk#:~:text=The%20commission%20would%20be%20tasked,report%20by%20November%201%2C%202027)). This timeline allows time to gather archival data, scholarly research, and public input. The emphasis on study and evidence – collecting data on economic harms, mapping descendants’ genealogy, quantifying wealth lost – shows a commitment to _systematically_ diagnose the problem before prescribing solutions. Such methodology aligns with an **algorithmic justice** lens, treating reparations as a process of using historical data to correct the “algorithm” of public policy that has until now produced racially biased outcomes. In fact, SB 587 explicitly calls for the study of policies that created disparities, essentially a forensic audit of Maryland’s legal code and economy through the lens of race ([House rejects GOP amendments, gives final approval to bill creating Reparations Commission - Maryland Matters](https://marylandmatters.org/2025/04/03/house-rejects-gop-amendments-gives-final-approval-to-bill-creating-reparations-commission/#:~:text=If%20approved%2C%20the%20commission%20would,policies%2C%E2%80%9D%20according%20to%20the%20bill)). By applying rigorous analysis, Maryland aims to develop reparations programs that are not arbitrary but precisely targeted to documented injustices – much as an algorithm might be designed to redress bias once the bias is measured.

Notably, the Black Caucus and other backers stress that reparations are _“not just about money.”_ The goal is transformational change: to restore what was stolen (wealth, opportunities, dignity) and to eliminate the structural racism that persists. The Commission’s very existence would be a form of acknowledgment. As Del. Stephanie Smith explained, the process is about _“offering just a conversation and a commission to acknowledge that [Black Marylanders] were here, that they lived, that they contributed.”_ ([House rejects GOP amendments, gives final approval to bill creating Reparations Commission - Maryland Matters](https://marylandmatters.org/2025/04/03/house-rejects-gop-amendments-gives-final-approval-to-bill-creating-reparations-commission/#:~:text=But%20Del.%20Stephanie%20Smith%20%28D,receive%20the%20benefits%20from%20them)) The official apologies and historical accounting recommended would serve a restorative function by formally recognizing Black Marylanders’ pain and contributions. This approach reflects a **decolonial** ethic: it seeks to rewrite the dominant historical narrative (which has often minimized or justified slavery and segregation) and instead center the experiences of the oppressed, validating their historical memory. In essence, Maryland’s Commission aims to decolonize the state’s history – telling the truth about slavery’s barbarity and Jim Crow’s cruelty – as a necessary step toward justice.

## Political Support and Opposition in the Era of Gov. Wes Moore

The political climate in Maryland in 2025 has been conducive to reparations advocacy in part due to leadership changes and grassroots pressure. Governor Wes Moore, the first Black governor in Maryland’s 246-year history, campaigned on themes of equity and closing opportunity gaps. While Gov. Moore did not explicitly push the reparations bill during the session, he has indicated he will give it serious consideration ([Maryland lawmakers approve measure to study reparations for slavery | AP News](https://apnews.com/article/slavery-reparations-maryland-wes-moore-2daea88df55e5f4b0053b124b042e145#:~:text=The%20101,he%20will%20consider%20the%20legislation)). His administration’s emphasis on economic justice and closing the racial wealth divide aligns philosophically with the bill’s aims. It is widely expected that Moore will sign SB 587, given his historic role and the bill’s overwhelming passage; doing so would underscore his commitment to making Maryland a leader in racial reconciliation. Indeed, Moore’s governorship itself is symbolic of Maryland’s evolving political landscape – one that increasingly centers diverse voices and confronts difficult history. Under Moore, the state created an Office of Equity and Inclusion and has pursued policies addressing poverty and education disparities, which complement the goals of the reparations commission. Implementing SB 587 would dovetail with Moore’s vision of “leaving no one behind,” providing a roadmap to materially invest in communities long left behind by state policy.

Within the General Assembly, support for SB 587 was driven by Democratic lawmakers (who hold large majorities), especially members of color representing Baltimore City and Prince George’s County – areas deeply affected by redlining and segregation. As noted, the Legislative Black Caucus unified around the bill, making it a centerpiece of their 90-day session agenda ([House rejects GOP amendments, gives final approval to bill creating Reparations Commission - Maryland Matters](https://marylandmatters.org/2025/04/03/house-rejects-gop-amendments-gives-final-approval-to-bill-creating-reparations-commission/#:~:text=It%20also%20helped%20that%2C%20for,the%20full%20chamber%20March%2014)). This unity and prioritization were critical in overcoming legislative inertia that had stalled past reparations proposals. White progressive lawmakers also backed the measure, framing it as a logical extension of Maryland’s efforts to reckon with its past (for example, the state had previously established a Truth and Reconciliation process for documented lynchings). Some descendants of slaveholding families in Maryland even voiced support, echoing a broader national trend of individuals confronting their ancestors’ legacies and advocating repair. The breadth of the coalition – from civil rights groups and church leaders to educators at HBCUs – signaled a maturation of the reparations debate from a fringe idea to a mainstream policy discussion in Maryland.

Despite broad support, opposition to the bill did emerge, primarily from Republican delegates who objected to the concept of reparations. During House debate, GOP lawmakers introduced amendments to narrow eligibility (for instance, excluding Black descendants who now live outside Maryland) or to halve the scope of payments ([Maryland bill to address slavery and reparations awaits governor's decision](https://san.com/cc/maryland-bill-to-address-slavery-and-reparations-awaits-governors-decision/#:~:text=)). These efforts were rejected. Opponents like Delegate Jason Buckel argued that _“We cannot remedy the entire problem of the United States of America”_ at a state level ([Maryland bill to address slavery and reparations awaits governor's decision](https://san.com/cc/maryland-bill-to-address-slavery-and-reparations-awaits-governors-decision/#:~:text=House%20lawmakers%20who%20opposed%20the,state%20should%20not%20be%20included)), suggesting reparations should be a federal matter if at all. Others claimed that it is unfair to “tax one race to give to another,” with Del. Matthew Morgan deriding the bill as creating a _“reparation tax”_ and calling equity _“a Marxist term”_ that would divide Americans ([House rejects GOP amendments, gives final approval to bill creating Reparations Commission - Maryland Matters](https://marylandmatters.org/2025/04/03/house-rejects-gop-amendments-gives-final-approval-to-bill-creating-reparations-commission/#:~:text=Critics%2C%20like%20Del,%E2%80%9D)). Such rhetoric reflects the enduring backlash whenever racial redress is proposed – fears of reopening old wounds or assigning collective guilt. However, supporters counter that society is already divided by the enduring effects of racism; reparations are a means to heal divides rather than create them. The moral counterargument was succinctly captured by Del. Smith’s reminder that Black Marylanders for generations paid into a system that denied them equal returns ([House rejects GOP amendments, gives final approval to bill creating Reparations Commission - Maryland Matters](https://marylandmatters.org/2025/04/03/house-rejects-gop-amendments-gives-final-approval-to-bill-creating-reparations-commission/#:~:text=But%20Del.%20Stephanie%20Smith%20%28D,receive%20the%20benefits%20from%20them)). In the end, the opposition was outvoted, but their concerns foreshadow challenges ahead: funding reparations and achieving broad public buy-in will require continued leadership and education. Governor Moore’s role will be pivotal in this regard – he will have to articulate to all Marylanders why this commission’s work benefits the entire state by promoting justice and reconciliation. If successful, Moore can help solidify a pro-reparations consensus in Maryland’s political culture, showing that acknowledging and investing in Black communities strengthens society as a whole.

## Evanston’s Housing Program: A Local Reparations Model

While Maryland pursues statewide reparations, it builds on examples set by smaller jurisdictions, notably **Evanston, Illinois**, which in 2019 became the first U.S. city to fund a reparations program. Evanston’s initiative, launched in 2021, specifically addresses the enduring harm from the city’s past housing discrimination. The **Local Reparations Restorative Housing Program** in Evanston provides eligible Black residents with up to **$25,000 grants** for housing purchases, home repairs, or mortgage assistance ([Evanston, Ill., Approves Historic Reparations Plan For Black Residents : NPR](https://www.npr.org/2021/03/23/980277688/in-likely-first-chicago-suburb-of-evanston-approves-reparations-for-black-reside#:~:text=Evanston%27s%20City%20Council%20voted%208,that%20was%20established%20in%202019)). The program’s design reflects the local history: Evanston had policies like redlining, restrictive covenants, and predatory lending that limited Black homeownership and forced Black families into a segregated district with lesser amenities. By offering housing grants, Evanston aims to directly counteract that legacy – increasing Black homeownership, keeping Black residents in the city, and ultimately building intergenerational wealth that was previously denied ([Evanston, Ill., Approves Historic Reparations Plan For Black Residents : NPR](https://www.npr.org/2021/03/23/980277688/in-likely-first-chicago-suburb-of-evanston-approves-reparations-for-black-reside#:~:text=Sponsor%20Message)). The reparations fund is not financed by general taxation but by dedicated revenue from the city’s tax on recreational cannabis sales ([Evanston, Ill., Approves Historic Reparations Plan For Black Residents : NPR](https://www.npr.org/2021/03/23/980277688/in-likely-first-chicago-suburb-of-evanston-approves-reparations-for-black-reside#:~:text=In%20November%202019%2C%20the%20City,is%20initially%20budgeted%20at%20%24400%2C000)). Evanston pledged the first $10 million of marijuana tax revenue to this fund, intentionally linking today’s social justice efforts to rectifying yesterday’s injustices (in this case, recognizing that Black residents were disproportionately harmed by criminalization of marijuana in the past and thus should benefit from its legalization windfall) ([Evanston, Ill., Approves Historic Reparations Plan For Black Residents : NPR](https://www.npr.org/2021/03/23/980277688/in-likely-first-chicago-suburb-of-evanston-approves-reparations-for-black-reside#:~:text=In%20November%202019%2C%20the%20City,is%20initially%20budgeted%20at%20%24400%2C000)).

Evanston’s program, though modest in scale, has been watched closely as a potential **national model** for municipal reparations ([Evanston, Ill., Approves Historic Reparations Plan For Black Residents : NPR](https://www.npr.org/2021/03/23/980277688/in-likely-first-chicago-suburb-of-evanston-approves-reparations-for-black-reside#:~:text=The%20city%20of%20Evanston%2C%20Ill,as%20a%20potential%20national%20model)). It demonstrates a pragmatic approach: rather than attempt reparations for every aspect of harm, the city targeted housing as both a critical need and a realm where the city itself had clear culpability. The Restorative Housing grants have begun to be disbursed, with a small initial cohort of Black senior citizens (many of whom had parents or grandparents victimized by housing discrimination) receiving funds to pay off mortgages or renovate long-time family homes. The impact on these families is tangible – for some, the $25,000 grant meant finally being able to secure stable housing or pass property to their children ([Evanston, Ill., Approves Historic Reparations Plan For Black Residents : NPR](https://www.npr.org/2021/03/23/980277688/in-likely-first-chicago-suburb-of-evanston-approves-reparations-for-black-reside#:~:text=Evanston%27s%20City%20Council%20voted%208,that%20was%20established%20in%202019)). Robin Rue Simmons, the former Evanston alderwoman who spearheaded the effort, framed it as a down payment on justice, not the culmination. _“We have a large and unfortunate gap in wealth, opportunity, education, even life expectancy [between Black and white residents],”_ she noted, and the reparations program is one step toward closing that gap ([Evanston, Ill., Approves Historic Reparations Plan For Black Residents : NPR](https://www.npr.org/2021/03/23/980277688/in-likely-first-chicago-suburb-of-evanston-approves-reparations-for-black-reside#:~:text=Robin%20Rue%20Simmons%2C%20an%20alderwoman,priced%20out%20of%20their%20homes)). In an Afrofuturist sense, Evanston’s initiative is about creating a future where Black residents can thrive in a city that once systematically excluded them – a future of restored community wellness and prosperity. It is also **decolonial** in that it forces the city to confront and atone for the racist policies embedded in its urban planning and real estate practices, essentially “decolonizing” the city’s development to include those it once displaced.

For Maryland, Evanston’s experience offers both inspiration and lessons. It shows that reparations can be pursued at a local level with creativity in funding and scope. Evanston’s focus on housing might guide Maryland’s Commission when considering remedies like housing assistance and anti-redlining measures. The community engagement in Evanston (public forums were held to shape the program) highlights the importance of including Black residents in designing reparations – a principle Maryland’s Commission is likely to follow through hearings and consultation. However, the scale is different: Maryland’s challenge is statewide and potentially involves vastly larger numbers of eligible individuals. Evanston’s $10 million commitment pales next to the breadth of harms across Maryland, but it establishes proof of concept that reparations can be done in a way that voters and stakeholders support. Notably, a Northwestern University survey found overwhelming support among Evanston’s residents for the reparations program after it was implemented, suggesting that tangible reparative action can build public goodwill ([Overwhelming support for Evanston reparations program, survey finds](https://news.northwestern.edu/stories/2023/10/reparations-survey-conducted-by-northwestern-center-reveals-overwhelming-community-support-for-evanston-program-across-every-ethnic-and-racial-demographic-group/#:~:text=Overwhelming%20support%20for%20Evanston%20reparations,funded%20reparations%20program)). Maryland will hope to similarly convince skeptics through the Commission’s thoughtful work and initial pilot projects that demonstrate positive outcomes.

## California’s Reparations Task Force: A Statewide Precedent

On the state level, Maryland’s bold foray into reparations joins the path carved by **California’s Reparations Task Force**, the first statewide body of its kind in the U.S. Created by legislation (AB 3121) signed in 2020, California’s task force was a nine-member panel charged with studying the harms of slavery and systemic discrimination in California and developing detailed reparations proposals. After two years of exhaustive research and public deliberation, the California Task Force issued a sweeping final report in June 2023, which Maryland’s Commission will no doubt scrutinize. The California recommendations outline a multi-faceted program including **direct compensation payments** to eligible Black Californians, a formal **state apology** for past wrongs, and over **100 policy reforms** to dismantle ongoing racial disparities ([Reparations panel gives lawmakers recommendations - CalMatters](https://calmatters.org/california-divide/2023/06/reparations-california/#:~:text=Key%20parts%20of%20the%20recommendations,redressing%20discrimination%20against%20African%20Americans)). The proposals cover a range of areas – from banning cash bail and ending racial over-policing, to investment in Black neighborhoods and curriculum changes in schools – reflecting the task force’s finding that the legacy of slavery touches nearly every aspect of life, from housing to healthcare. Significantly, the task force recommended that eligibility for California’s reparations be based on **lineage** (descent from enslaved or free Black people in the U.S. prior to 1900) rather than race per se ([Reparations panel gives lawmakers recommendations - CalMatters](https://calmatters.org/california-divide/2023/06/reparations-california/#:~:text=One%20persistent%20debate%20was%20over,States%20before%201900%20would%20qualify)). This lineage-based standard was intended to target those historically impacted while hopefully passing legal muster (to avoid being struck down as a race-based program). Maryland’s bill similarly centers descendants of the enslaved and victims of specific policies, which may shield it from legal challenges that have thwarted race-conscious remedies in the past ([House rejects GOP amendments, gives final approval to bill creating Reparations Commission - Maryland Matters](https://marylandmatters.org/2025/04/03/house-rejects-gop-amendments-gives-final-approval-to-bill-creating-reparations-commission/#:~:text=If%20approved%2C%20the%20commission%20would,policies%2C%E2%80%9D%20according%20to%20the%20bill)).

One headline-grabbing aspect of California’s effort was its attempt to **quantify financial harm** in concrete terms – essentially an algorithmic accounting of historical damages. Economists working with the task force calculated various categories of loss: for example, they estimated that the economic effects of **housing discrimination** in California (such as redlining and racist zoning, 1933–1977) cost each affected Black resident roughly $3,378 for each year they lived in the state during that period ([California's slavery reparations plan: Eligibility, payments and more - Los Angeles Times](https://www.latimes.com/california/story/2023-06-29/la-me-california-slavery-reparations-plan#:~:text=,drugs%20from%201971%20to%202020)). Likewise, the impacts of **over-policing and mass incarceration** during the “War on Drugs” era (1971–2020) were valued at about $2,352 per person for each year resident in California in that period ([California's slavery reparations plan: Eligibility, payments and more - Los Angeles Times](https://www.latimes.com/california/story/2023-06-29/la-me-california-slavery-reparations-plan#:~:text=,Hispanic%20Californians)). Health disparities stemming from segregation and unequal access were another category, with an estimated $13,619 per year of life in California (from 1850–2020) to account for the lower life expectancy and greater disease burden in Black communities ([California's slavery reparations plan: Eligibility, payments and more - Los Angeles Times](https://www.latimes.com/california/story/2023-06-29/la-me-california-slavery-reparations-plan#:~:text=The%20pay%20models%20suggest%3A)). These per-year figures, when summed for an individual who lived a long life in California, led to headlines suggesting reparations payments could total in the hundreds of thousands or even over $1 million for some individuals ([What to know about California's approval of recommendations for ...](https://abcnews.go.com/US/californias-approval-recommendations-historic-reparations/story?id=99253083#:~:text=What%20to%20know%20about%20California%27s,million%20to%20eligible%20Black%20residents)) ([California slavery reparations: payments up to $1.2m/person | Fortune](https://fortune.com/2023/06/07/california-plans-pay-reparations-slavery-legacy-payments-12-million-person/#:~:text=California%20slavery%20reparations%3A%20payments%20up,2m%20per%20person)). In reality, the task force did **not** set a single dollar amount per person; instead it offered these models to illustrate the scale of reparations that would be justified if compensation were commensurate with documented loss ([Reparations panel gives lawmakers recommendations - CalMatters](https://calmatters.org/california-divide/2023/06/reparations-california/#:~:text=If%20adopted%20the%20recommendations%20could,owed%20to%20most%20Black%20Californians)). The task force ultimately urged the California legislature to **issue “down payments”** to begin restitution, prioritizing elderly survivors first, and to establish an ongoing state agency (the California African American Freedmen Affairs Agency) to implement the programs and verify lineage claims ([California's slavery reparations plan: Eligibility, payments and more - Los Angeles Times](https://www.latimes.com/california/story/2023-06-29/la-me-california-slavery-reparations-plan#:~:text=,which%20the%20Legislature%20would%20quantify)) ([California's slavery reparations plan: Eligibility, payments and more - Los Angeles Times](https://www.latimes.com/california/story/2023-06-29/la-me-california-slavery-reparations-plan#:~:text=,according%20to%20the%20final%20report)). In June 2023, the task force formally approved its final recommendations including the call for a **public apology** from the state of California acknowledging its responsibility for centuries of racism and pledging non-repetition ([California reparations panel OKs state apology, payments | AP News](https://apnews.com/article/california-reparations-task-force-apology-de0b43dd237c47113554a9aae56ca9f5#:~:text=match%20at%20L1344%20The%20task,people%20whose%20ancestors%20were%20enslaved)). This apology, the task force insisted, must be delivered in a meaningful way – _“in the presence of people whose ancestors were enslaved,”_ and include a candid admission of the state’s past barbarities ([California reparations panel OKs state apology, payments | AP News](https://apnews.com/article/california-reparations-task-force-apology-de0b43dd237c47113554a9aae56ca9f5#:~:text=An%20apology%20crafted%20by%20lawmakers,approved%20by%20the%20task%20force)).

California’s experience offers a valuable blueprint and some cautionary notes for Maryland. The comprehensive scope of California’s recommendations – from cash payments to policy guarantees of non-repetition – underscores that reparations are not a one-time payout but a multi-dimensional program. Maryland’s Commission is explicitly tasked with looking at policy changes as well as monetary compensation ([House rejects GOP amendments, gives final approval to bill creating Reparations Commission - Maryland Matters](https://marylandmatters.org/2025/04/03/house-rejects-gop-amendments-gives-final-approval-to-bill-creating-reparations-commission/#:~:text=The%20commission%20would%20also%20examine,incentives%20and%20child%20care%20costs)), much like California’s approach. For instance, California’s proposals to repeal certain laws (such as Proposition 209, which banned affirmative action in public institutions ([Reparations panel gives lawmakers recommendations - CalMatters](https://calmatters.org/california-divide/2023/06/reparations-california/#:~:text=Reparations%20panel%20gives%20lawmakers%20recommendations,209%20to%20funding%20free))) indicate that sometimes removing contemporary barriers is part of reparative justice. Maryland might consider its own legal reforms (for example, strengthening Black business procurement programs or reforming criminal justice practices) as part of a reparations package. On the fiscal side, California’s legislature has been cautious in responding to the task force – as of early 2025, no broad reparations law has been enacted, and Governor Gavin Newsom has cited budgetary and political complexities, though he expressed moral support ([Reparations panel gives lawmakers recommendations - CalMatters](https://calmatters.org/california-divide/2023/06/reparations-california/#:~:text=It%E2%80%99s%20unclear%20how%20those%20recommendations,will%20be%20received)) ([Reparations panel gives lawmakers recommendations - CalMatters](https://calmatters.org/california-divide/2023/06/reparations-california/#:~:text=Thursday%2C%20while%20taking%20questions%20from,%E2%80%9D)). This reveals a challenge Maryland will also face: moving from recommendation to implementation, especially if price tags are high. However, California’s task force has significantly furthered public discourse. It normalized conversations about state responsibility for historical crimes and, importantly, provided a trove of research that other states can leverage. Maryland’s Commission will stand on the shoulders of this work, able to use evidence from California’s report (which runs over 500 pages) to bolster its own findings. The California panel demonstrated the value of an **Africana studies** approach – grounding policy proposals in rigorous historical and sociological research about the African American experience. Maryland’s effort, similarly, is expected to be rooted in **Africana studies** scholarship, perhaps partnering with local universities like Morgan State or Howard (in nearby D.C.) to conduct research. By applying an **algorithmic justice** framework to quantify harm (as California did with its damage formulas) and an **Afrofuturist** outlook to imagine a repaired future for Black citizens, Maryland’s Commission can build on California’s precedent to create something tailored to Maryland’s unique history and needs.

## Federal Context: Tulsa, the DOJ Findings, and the Greenwood Project

Maryland’s reparations push is part of a larger national awakening that includes revisiting some of the most horrific racial atrocities in U.S. history. A poignant example is the renewed pursuit of justice for the **1921 Tulsa Race Massacre**. In 2024, the U.S. Department of Justice (DOJ) released landmark findings on the Tulsa massacre, correcting the historical record and affirming what Black Tulsans long knew: the destruction of Tulsa’s Greenwood district was not a spontaneous “race riot” but a **“coordinated, military-style attack”** on a Black community ([Justice Department Finds Tulsa Massacre Was a “Coordinated, Military-Style Attack”](https://eji.org/news/justice-department-finds-tulsa-massacre-was-a-coordinated-military-style-attack/#:~:text=1921%2C%20when%20as%20many%20as,%E2%80%9D)). Federal investigators, after a detailed review, concluded the white mob’s actions were so systematic that they _“transcended mere mob violence,”_ involving organized forces that killed hundreds of Black residents and burned 35 blocks of Black-owned homes and businesses to the ground ([Justice Department Finds Tulsa Massacre Was a “Coordinated, Military-Style Attack”](https://eji.org/news/justice-department-finds-tulsa-massacre-was-a-coordinated-military-style-attack/#:~:text=1921%2C%20when%20as%20many%20as,%E2%80%9D)) ( [Office of Public Affairs | Justice Department Announces Results of Review and Evaluation of the Tulsa Race Massacre | United States Department of Justice](https://www.justice.gov/archives/opa/pr/justice-department-announces-results-review-and-evaluation-tulsa-race-massacre#:~:text=Violence%20was%20initially%20unorganized%20and,across%20and%20beyond%20the%20city)). This DOJ report, released in January 2025, carries significant weight – it is the first time the federal government formally acknowledged the true nature of the Tulsa massacre as a large-scale coordinated atrocity, effectively a **racial pogrom**. Assistant Attorney General Kristen Clarke noted that for over a century, the federal government had been largely silent about this “darkest episode” in American history, but the report _“breaks that silence”_ and lays bare new information, including how Tulsa law enforcement not only failed to protect Black citizens but actively participated in the violence ([Justice Department Finds Tulsa Massacre Was a “Coordinated, Military-Style Attack”](https://eji.org/news/justice-department-finds-tulsa-massacre-was-a-coordinated-military-style-attack/#:~:text=%E2%80%9CThe%20Tulsa%20race%20massacre%20stands,%E2%80%9D)) ( [Office of Public Affairs | Justice Department Announces Results of Review and Evaluation of the Tulsa Race Massacre | United States Department of Justice](https://www.justice.gov/archives/opa/pr/justice-department-announces-results-review-and-evaluation-tulsa-race-massacre#:~:text=challenge%20to%20the%20social%20order,eventually%20ravage%20the%20Greenwood%20community)). The DOJ’s findings strengthen the moral and legal case for reparations related to Tulsa, even as they acknowledge that criminal prosecution is no longer possible due to the passage of time and death of perpetrators ([Tulsa’s new mayor backs ‘significant elements’ of plan for city to do more over Tulsa Race Massacre - WTOP News](https://wtop.com/national/2025/02/tulsas-new-mayor-backs-significant-elements-of-plan-for-city-to-do-more-over-tulsa-race-massacre/#:~:text=In%20January%2C%20a%20report%20released,criminal%20prosecution%20over%20the%20massacre)). In a broader sense, this federal action is part of what one might call a **decolonizing of public memory** – an official unmasking of past racial terror that had been minimized or rationalized in mainstream narratives.

In Tulsa, Oklahoma, these revelations have galvanized local efforts for reparative justice, embodied in a comprehensive proposal known as **Project Greenwood**. This plan, championed by survivors, descendants, and advocates like Justice for Greenwood, seeks to obtain some measure of repair for the Tulsa Race Massacre. After the Oklahoma Supreme Court in 2024 dismissively **rejected a lawsuit** by the last three known survivors (Viola Fletcher, 110; Lessie Benningfield Randle, 109; and a third who has since passed) on narrow procedural grounds ([Maryland Reparations Bill Head To Gov. Wes Moore For Approval](https://www.blackenterprise.com/reparations-bill-maryland/#:~:text=This%20renewed%20focus%20on%20reparations,the%20state%E2%80%99s%20public%20nuisance%20laws)) ([Tulsa’s new mayor backs ‘significant elements’ of plan for city to do more over Tulsa Race Massacre - WTOP News](https://wtop.com/national/2025/02/tulsas-new-mayor-backs-significant-elements-of-plan-for-city-to-do-more-over-tulsa-race-massacre/#:~:text=The%20sweeping%20plan%20is%20pushed,overseen%20by%20the%20National%20Guard)), proponents shifted strategies from the courtroom to City Hall. In late 2024 and early 2025, they crafted Project Greenwood as a package of community-based reparations and put pressure on Tulsa’s city leadership to adopt it. A breakthrough came with the election of **Monroe Nichols** in November 2024 as Tulsa’s first Black mayor. In February 2025, Mayor Nichols publicly declared support for “significant elements” of Project Greenwood, indicating a willingness to partner with survivors and descendants to implement the plan ([Tulsa’s new mayor backs ‘significant elements’ of plan for city to do more over Tulsa Race Massacre - WTOP News](https://wtop.com/national/2025/02/tulsas-new-mayor-backs-significant-elements-of-plan-for-city-to-do-more-over-tulsa-race-massacre/#:~:text=Monroe%20Nichols%2C%20who%20in%20November,destroyed%20by%20a%20white%20mob)) ([Tulsa’s new mayor backs ‘significant elements’ of plan for city to do more over Tulsa Race Massacre - WTOP News](https://wtop.com/national/2025/02/tulsas-new-mayor-backs-significant-elements-of-plan-for-city-to-do-more-over-tulsa-race-massacre/#:~:text=%E2%80%9CI%20look%20forward%20to%20implementing,create%20a%20stronger%2C%20more%20unified)). The **Greenwood Project** (often called “Project Greenwood”) includes several key components aimed at truth, memory, and material repair: it calls for direct **financial compensation** to the two remaining survivors, the establishment of a **scholarship program** for descendants of massacre victims, and the creation of an official **city holiday on June 1** to commemorate the massacre and educate future generations ([Tulsa’s new mayor backs ‘significant elements’ of plan for city to do more over Tulsa Race Massacre - WTOP News](https://wtop.com/national/2025/02/tulsas-new-mayor-backs-significant-elements-of-plan-for-city-to-do-more-over-tulsa-race-massacre/#:~:text=The%20proposals%20in%20Project%20Greenwood,June%201%20an%20official%20holiday)). Additionally, it proposes systemic changes like preference programs for descendants in city hiring and contracting, exemptions from city taxes and utility fees for descendants, and a thorough audit of land ownership in the Greenwood area to address historical land theft ([Tulsa’s new mayor backs ‘significant elements’ of plan for city to do more over Tulsa Race Massacre - WTOP News](https://wtop.com/national/2025/02/tulsas-new-mayor-backs-significant-elements-of-plan-for-city-to-do-more-over-tulsa-race-massacre/#:~:text=Other%20proposals%20in%20the%20plan,and%20how%20it%20was%20obtained)). Essentially, Project Greenwood is a city-level reparations blueprint responding to a specific historical atrocity, incorporating both symbolic measures (memorialization and apology) and substantive ones (payments and economic opportunities).

The connection between the DOJ findings and Project Greenwood is profound. The federal acknowledgement of Tulsa as a coordinated attack validates the claims of survivors and descendants, who for decades were told their community’s destruction was just an inevitable riot or even blamed on Black resistance. By officially affirming that it was _mass murder and arson with government complicity_, the DOJ has provided a powerful narrative tool that Greenwood advocates can leverage. For example, the DOJ report documents how Tulsa authorities deputized white mobs and even held Black survivors in internment camps ( [Office of Public Affairs | Justice Department Announces Results of Review and Evaluation of the Tulsa Race Massacre | United States Department of Justice](https://www.justice.gov/archives/opa/pr/justice-department-announces-results-review-and-evaluation-tulsa-race-massacre#:~:text=challenge%20to%20the%20social%20order,eventually%20ravage%20the%20Greenwood%20community)) ( [Office of Public Affairs | Justice Department Announces Results of Review and Evaluation of the Tulsa Race Massacre | United States Department of Justice](https://www.justice.gov/archives/opa/pr/justice-department-announces-results-review-and-evaluation-tulsa-race-massacre#:~:text=taking%20into%20custody%20men%2C%20women%2C,left%20with%20little%20to%20nothing)). These facts bolster calls for the City of Tulsa and state of Oklahoma to accept responsibility and act. Mayor Nichols’ willingness to engage with Project Greenwood likely stems partly from this changed context – the highest law enforcement agency in the country essentially sided with the truth long voiced by Black Tulsans. In this way, the Tulsa case illustrates a model of **federal-local synergy** in pursuing reparations: the federal government corrects the record and perhaps lays groundwork (moral and factual), while local leaders then implement reparative solutions on the ground. It is noteworthy that the DOJ’s role stops short of enforcement (they found no current avenue for legal prosecution ([Tulsa’s new mayor backs ‘significant elements’ of plan for city to do more over Tulsa Race Massacre - WTOP News](https://wtop.com/national/2025/02/tulsas-new-mayor-backs-significant-elements-of-plan-for-city-to-do-more-over-tulsa-race-massacre/#:~:text=In%20January%2C%20a%20report%20released,criminal%20prosecution%20over%20the%20massacre))), so it falls to local government to do justice by other means. In Tulsa, that means political action. In Maryland’s case, the federal context is less direct but still relevant – the Biden Administration’s supportive stance on racial equity and investigations like Tulsa’s create a climate in which state-level reparations efforts have greater legitimacy.

Maryland’s reparations commission will likely take inspiration from Tulsa’s ongoing fight, especially in how to handle cases where time and legal loopholes have denied justice. The Tulsa massacre and Maryland slavery are different historical crimes, but both involve **official complicity** and **century-long denials**. Just as Tulsa’s truth is coming to light after 100 years, Maryland’s commission is an attempt to confront 250 years of slavery (from the 1600s to 1864 in Maryland) and another century of systemic racism thereafter. Both efforts reflect a broader national movement of truth-telling and reconciliation. As a further parallel, Tulsa’s Project Greenwood and Maryland’s SB 587 share a focus on **education and commemoration**: Tulsa wants a massacre memorial day (June 1) and scholarships in victims’ names ([Tulsa’s new mayor backs ‘significant elements’ of plan for city to do more over Tulsa Race Massacre - WTOP News](https://wtop.com/national/2025/02/tulsas-new-mayor-backs-significant-elements-of-plan-for-city-to-do-more-over-tulsa-race-massacre/#:~:text=The%20proposals%20in%20Project%20Greenwood,June%201%20an%20official%20holiday)), while Maryland’s bill mentions formal apologies and likely will include educational curricula about slavery and racism in its recommendations. By honoring the ancestors and educating descendants, these initiatives adopt an Afrofuturist outlook – remembering the past to inform a more just future, and empowering young Black generations with knowledge and resources to thrive. The resonance between Tulsa and Maryland underlines that reparations, whether for a single horrific massacre or centuries of widespread oppression, are fundamentally about recognition, repair, and a commitment that such injustices _“will not repeat”_ ([California reparations panel OKs state apology, payments | AP News](https://apnews.com/article/california-reparations-task-force-apology-de0b43dd237c47113554a9aae56ca9f5#:~:text=The%20task%20force%20approved%20a,people%20whose%20ancestors%20were%20enslaved)).

## SB 587 in National and Global Reparations Movements

The movement for reparations has accelerated in recent years, not only in Maryland and California but across the United States and even internationally. Maryland’s SB 587 can be seen as part of a **broader national trajectory** in which local and state governments are stepping up to address what the federal government has long hesitated to do. On the national stage, legislation for a U.S. **Reparations Study Commission** (H.R. 40 in Congress) has been introduced repeatedly since 1989, but despite growing support, it has not yet been enacted. In 2021, H.R. 40 was voted out of committee in the House for the first time, indicating momentum, but it stalled before reaching a full vote ([Maryland lawmakers approve measure to study reparations for slavery](https://apnews.com/article/slavery-reparations-maryland-wes-moore-2daea88df55e5f4b0053b124b042e145#:~:text=slavery%20apnews.com%20%20The%20101,he%20will%20consider%20the%20legislation)). With Congress paralyzed on this issue, states have taken the initiative. As noted, Maryland would become only the fourth state to create an official reparations commission ([House rejects GOP amendments, gives final approval to bill creating Reparations Commission - Maryland Matters](https://marylandmatters.org/2025/04/03/house-rejects-gop-amendments-gives-final-approval-to-bill-creating-reparations-commission/#:~:text=The%20101,and%20New%20York%20in%202023)). Other states like _New Jersey_ and _New York_ are considering similar bodies, and a task force in _Colorado_ has been exploring reparations at the state level ([Lawmakers pass bill to study reparations for ancestors of Maryland slavery](https://www.wmar2news.com/infocus/maryland-reparations-commission-bill-sent-to-gov-moores-desk#:~:text=Lawmakers%20say%20similar%20commissions%20were,Illinois%2C%20Massachusetts%2C%20and%20New%20York)). In addition, numerous cities and counties – beyond Evanston – have launched reparations task forces or pilot programs. For instance, Chicago, Providence (Rhode Island), Asheville (North Carolina), and San Francisco are among the municipalities that convened commissions to study local reparations for slavery or segregation. Maryland’s effort both learns from and adds to this patchwork: by tackling reparations at the state level in a former slave state, Maryland is breaking ground. It sends a signal that reparations are not just a West Coast or small-city phenomenon but have relevance in the Mid-Atlantic, in the very soil of America’s original colonies. This could inspire neighboring states like Virginia or Pennsylvania to consider their own histories of slavery and segregation.

Importantly, Maryland’s commission is tasked with reviewing reparations efforts _nationwide_, explicitly to gather best practices and insights ([MD SB587 - BillTrack50](https://www.billtrack50.com/billdetail/1806380#:~:text=MD%20SB587%20,efforts%20nationwide%2C%20exploring%20potential)). In doing so, it will be connecting to a network of grassroots and governmental initiatives. This exchange of ideas is creating a de-facto national movement from the ground up. Even without a federal mandate, a mosaic of local actions can advance the cause. For example, Illinois (another state with a commission) has the **Abrahamic Descendants Reparations Commission** working on recommendations ([House rejects GOP amendments, gives final approval to bill creating Reparations Commission - Maryland Matters](https://marylandmatters.org/2025/04/03/house-rejects-gop-amendments-gives-final-approval-to-bill-creating-reparations-commission/#:~:text=The%20101,and%20New%20York%20in%202023)), and cities like Detroit are considering direct payments to residents affected by housing discrimination. Each of these informs the others. Conferences and symposiums on reparations have proliferated, often invoking the philosophy of restorative justice and sharing economic models. Maryland’s representatives have noted that similar commissions or committees have been created in **“California, Colorado, Illinois, Massachusetts, and New York”**, underscoring that SB 587 fits into a mounting wave of public policy addressing historical inequality ([Lawmakers pass bill to study reparations for ancestors of Maryland slavery](https://www.wmar2news.com/infocus/maryland-reparations-commission-bill-sent-to-gov-moores-desk#:~:text=Lawmakers%20say%20similar%20commissions%20were,Illinois%2C%20Massachusetts%2C%20and%20New%20York)). This trend reflects a shifting public consciousness: opinion polls show growing, though still mixed, support for the idea of studying reparations nationally, especially among younger Americans. The involvement of diverse stakeholders (for instance, faith groups, universities, and even some corporations) suggests reparations are leaving the realm of taboo and entering practical discourse.

On the **global stage**, the reparations movement is even older and in some ways more advanced. Many former colonies and marginalized groups worldwide have been seeking reparative justice for historical crimes such as slavery, colonialism, and genocide. Maryland’s initiative, though focused on American slavery, resonates with these international efforts, aligning with what UN Secretary-General António Guterres recently called _“reparatory justice frameworks to help overcome generations of exclusion and discrimination.”_ ([UN chief calls for slavery reparations to overcome 'generations of discrimination' | Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/world/un-chief-calls-slavery-reparations-overcome-generations-discrimination-2024-03-25/#:~:text=Sign%20up%20here)) In March 2024, Guterres explicitly urged nations to consider reparations for the transatlantic slave trade and slavery, linking it to dismantling systemic racism today ([UN chief calls for slavery reparations to overcome 'generations of discrimination' | Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/world/un-chief-calls-slavery-reparations-overcome-generations-discrimination-2024-03-25/#:~:text=March%2025%20%28Reuters%29%20,today%27s%20society%2C%20including%20systemic%20racism)) ([UN chief calls for slavery reparations to overcome 'generations of discrimination' | Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/world/un-chief-calls-slavery-reparations-overcome-generations-discrimination-2024-03-25/#:~:text=,Guterres%20said)). This high-level endorsement at the United Nations reflects how reparations are increasingly seen as a global human rights issue, not just a national political choice. For instance, the countries of the **Caribbean Community (CARICOM)** have formed a Reparations Commission that is actively pressing European former colonial powers (like the UK, France, Spain, and the Netherlands) for reparations for slavery and native genocide. CARICOM’s strategy includes seeking debt cancellation, direct investments in development, and public apologies from Europe ([UN chief calls for slavery reparations to overcome 'generations of discrimination' | Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/world/un-chief-calls-slavery-reparations-overcome-generations-discrimination-2024-03-25/#:~:text=humanity%2C%20of%20good%20over%20evil%2C,General%20Assembly)). As the chair of the CARICOM Reparations Commission, Sir Hilary Beckles, stated at the UN, this is part of a _“global movement”_ which he believes will mark _“the collective victory of humanity, of good over evil.”_ ([UN chief calls for slavery reparations to overcome 'generations of discrimination' | Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/world/un-chief-calls-slavery-reparations-overcome-generations-discrimination-2024-03-25/#:~:text=momentum%20worldwide)) His framing casts reparations as not only material compensation but a moral victory in the long struggle against white supremacy and colonial exploitation. African nations and diaspora communities are also involved – for example, Nigeria and Ghana have at times supported diaspora calls for reparations, and the African Union in collaboration with CARICOM has even discussed a **Global Reparations Fund** ([Global Reparation Fund - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Reparation_Fund#:~:text=Global%20Reparation%20Fund%20,their%20former%20European%20colonial%20powers)). In Europe, there are parallel debates: France has acknowledged slavery as a crime against humanity (though stopped short of reparations), the Netherlands in 2022 formally apologized for its role in slavery and set up a fund for awareness projects, and institutions like the Church of England have committed funds to atone for benefitting from the slave trade.

Maryland’s SB 587, while focused on state-level harms, is part of this larger tapestry. It embodies the principles that global advocates assert: acknowledgment of historical wrongdoing, compensation or repair for those wrongs, and guarantees of non-recurrence (e.g., through educational reforms and policy changes). By adopting a **decolonial lens**, Maryland is in step with global movements that seek to dismantle the lingering structures of colonialism and slavery. Decolonial theory emphasizes understanding how historical systems of oppression (like colonial slavery) continue to shape present inequalities, and then taking action to undo those structures. SB 587’s comprehensive approach – studying history, educating the public, and recommending forward-looking remedies – echoes that methodology. Furthermore, Maryland’s effort can draw on international examples for innovative ideas. For instance, some Caribbean nations have called for technology transfer and climate resilience support as forms of reparations, recognizing that the consequences of slavery and colonial extraction are also environmental and technological. An **Afrofuturist perspective** encourages reimagining what Black communities’ futures could look like if truly repaired and empowered – perhaps a future where communities are thriving, technologically advanced, and free from the algorithmic biases that currently plague areas like finance, healthcare, and criminal justice. In this sense, reparations can be seen as fueling a brighter future, not just settling the debts of the past. Maryland’s Commission might engage with these ideas, ensuring that its proposals are not just backward-looking (compensating for harm) but also forward-looking (investing in Black advancement, innovation, and leadership for generations to come).

## Impact and Afrofuturist Vision for Maryland’s Black Communities

If Maryland’s reparations commission proceeds as planned and its recommendations translate into action, the potential impacts on Black communities in Maryland could be profound and lasting. In tangible terms, eligible Black Marylanders may receive financial benefits such as debt relief, housing assistance, or tuition support that directly improve their economic stability. For example, a family that has struggled to buy a home due to lack of intergenerational wealth might, through the program, receive a down payment grant or property tax relief that finally puts homeownership within reach ([Maryland Reparations Bill Head To Gov. Wes Moore For Approval](https://www.blackenterprise.com/reparations-bill-maryland/#:~:text=The%20reparations%20package%20could%20include,tuition%20waivers%20for%20higher%20education)). This in turn can start building equity for that family, breaking a cycle of renting and wealth stagnation. Similarly, young people who are descendants of the enslaved may get scholarships or tuition waivers, enabling them to graduate college debt-free ([Maryland Reparations Bill Head To Gov. Wes Moore For Approval](https://www.blackenterprise.com/reparations-bill-maryland/#:~:text=The%20reparations%20package%20could%20include,tuition%20waivers%20for%20higher%20education)). The ripple effects of higher education and homeownership – two pillars of middle-class security in America – mean that reparations could significantly narrow the racial wealth gap in Maryland over time. Social services like subsidized childcare and healthcare (if included in the final package) would directly improve quality of life, freeing up household income and reducing stress for Black families who have often faced the highest barriers to such services. In short, the material aid from reparations can uplift individuals and households, while also strengthening entire communities as more members gain financial footing and invest locally.

Beyond the direct benefits, one must consider the **psychological and social impact** of a state formally acknowledging and atoning for historical wrongs. Such recognition can be deeply healing. Generations of Black Marylanders have endured the intergenerational trauma of slavery and racial terror – psychological wounds often compounded by the lack of validation of their suffering. A formal apology from the State of Maryland for slavery and segregation, as envisioned by SB 587 ([Lawmakers pass bill to study reparations for ancestors of Maryland slavery](https://www.wmar2news.com/infocus/maryland-reparations-commission-bill-sent-to-gov-moores-desk#:~:text=Some%20of%20the%20reparations%20to,debt%20forgiveness%2C%20and%20college%20tuition)), could be a powerful moment of validation. It would appear in the history books and archives that the state admits its role in atrocities and asks forgiveness. This is not mere symbolism; in transitional justice processes around the world (such as truth commissions in South Africa or Rwanda), official acknowledgment is a critical step in restoring dignity to victims. In Maryland, descendants of enslaved people might feel a measure of closure knowing that the government has finally said, “What was done to your ancestors was wrong, and we owe you a debt.” The creation of memorials or inclusion of Maryland’s slavery history in school curricula (likely recommendations from the Commission) would ensure that future generations remember and honor the struggles of Black Marylanders, further cementing the dignity and historical agency of the community.

Politically, enacting reparations would make Maryland a pioneer and could alter the state’s racial climate in positive ways. Under Gov. Wes Moore’s leadership, the implementation of any reparations plan would send a message that Maryland is earnestly confronting racism, not just through rhetoric but action. This can build greater trust between Black communities and the state government. Governor Moore has emphasized unity and equity in his administration; supporting the reparations commission’s work would demonstrate that those principles are being applied boldly. It might also energize Moore’s base and legacy – he could go down in history as the governor who shepherded one of the most significant racial justice initiatives of the 21st century in Maryland. At the same time, there is the potential for backlash or misuse of the issue in the political arena. Detractors might attempt to stoke resentment among white citizens by mischaracterizing reparations as taking something away from one group to give to another. How Moore and other leaders handle the public narrative will be crucial. If they highlight stories of Black elders finally receiving recognition, or Black youth getting opportunities that benefit the whole society (for instance, more college-educated citizens joining the workforce helps the economy broadly), they can frame reparations as a collective win. Indeed, an _Afrofuturist lens_ would encourage framing reparations as an investment in a shared future – one where Black Marylanders are not hindered by the shadows of the past and can contribute even more robustly to the state’s prosperity and creativity. In such a future, we might imagine predominantly Black neighborhoods in Baltimore or Prince George’s County blossoming with new businesses, cultural institutions, and tech hubs, fueled in part by reparative investments made today.

There is also an **algorithmic justice** component to the impact, in the sense that data and technology can be harnessed to ensure the reparations process itself is fair and effective. For instance, Maryland could develop transparent algorithms to calculate individual eligibility or benefit levels based on archival records (e.g. helping people trace lineage to Maryland slaves via digitized records, similar to the genealogical research assistance recommended in California ([California's slavery reparations plan: Eligibility, payments and more - Los Angeles Times](https://www.latimes.com/california/story/2023-06-29/la-me-california-slavery-reparations-plan#:~:text=,according%20to%20the%20final%20report))). By doing so, the state can avoid bureaucratic bias and make the process accessible. Moreover, algorithmic tools could monitor the outcomes of reparations programs – tracking metrics like Black homeownership rates, income levels, educational attainment, health outcomes – to objectively evaluate if the interventions are closing gaps as intended. In an ideal scenario, Maryland’s approach to reparations could become a prototype for using **data science in service of racial justice**, continually adjusting programs for maximum equity impact. This would embody the concept of algorithmic justice: using the same computational rigor that often perpetuates bias (through biased algorithms) but flipping it to dismantle bias and promote fairness.

From a decolonial and Afrofuturist standpoint, the ultimate impact of Maryland’s reparations initiative could be a reimagining of societal structures. Decolonial theory in practice means that institutions (schools, banks, courts) would examine and purge practices that echo colonial or racist logics. For example, Maryland’s education system might incorporate African and African American studies more centrally (so that the narrative of Black Marylanders is told by themselves, not the colonizer’s perspective). Economically, one could envision cooperative models or community land trusts being supported – ideas that break from capitalist exploitative paradigms and instead build collective wealth in a community, aligning with indigenous and African communal traditions. An Afrofuturist vision, meanwhile, would encourage Black Marylanders to envision futures not limited by current inequities: perhaps Black-led tech startups in Maryland pioneering algorithms free of bias, or urban planning that bridges formerly redlined areas with green, smart development driven by community input. Reparations, if successful, are not an end but a beginning – they seed the ground for these futures to take root. By materially compensating for the past and systemically preventing future harm, Maryland can empower Black residents to be full co-creators in the state’s future.

In conclusion, Maryland Senate Bill 587 represents a bold stride toward justice that is at once restorative and radically hopeful. It looks back unflinchingly at a painful past – slavery, segregation, racial terror – and insists that this past be accounted for through meaningful action. At the same time, it looks forward to an inclusive future, one that a scholar of Africana studies or an Afrofuturist novelist alike might recognize as part of the long Black freedom struggle’s dream: a future where reparations have helped to level the playing field, allowing Black excellence and innovation to flourish unencumbered. Whether in the form of a check, a scholarship, a healed memory, or an inspiring new business on a once-neglected Baltimore block, the impact of reparations in Maryland will echo the rallying cry seen scrawled on a city street corner – **“Make America Pay Reparations”** – not out of vengeance, but as payment on a debt long overdue, to enable a brighter, more equitable tomorrow for all.

# Maryland Senate Bill 587 (2025): Reparations in a Decolonial Afrofuturist Frame

## 📌 Overview of Maryland’s Reparations Bill

Maryland’s Senate Bill 587 (2025) marks a landmark effort to address the enduring harms of slavery and institutional racism. The Legislative Black Caucus of Maryland championed this bill, which passed the House of Delegates by 101–36 and now heads to Governor Wes Moore’s desk. If signed into law, SB 587 would establish a Maryland Reparations Commission empowered to study and recommend “appropriate benefits to be made” to those whose ancestors were enslaved in Maryland or who were harmed by inequitable government policies. Eligible recipients are defined as individuals of African descent with Maryland enslaved ancestors or who have suffered from systemic discrimination in the state. In other words, the reparations scope is deliberately broad, encompassing both the descendants of enslaved people and those directly impacted by Jim Crow-era policies of segregation and exclusion.

SB 587 outlines a comprehensive suite of reparative measures aimed at redress in multiple domains – monetary, social, housing, and educational. Rather than a single payout, the legislation envisions reparations as a package of supports to uplift Black communities. Possible remedies include official statements of apology from the state, direct monetary compensation, property tax rebates, and expanded social services assistance. The bill also proposes alleviating economic barriers by waiving or reimbursing certain fees (such as business licensing and permit fees) and providing help with home down payments for first-time buyers. Additional support under consideration features small business incentives, subsidized childcare, debt forgiveness, and tuition waivers for higher education.

Delegate Aletheia McCaskill emphasized that reparations would come in multiple forms beyond financial payments. The commission will “explore strategies to support and uplift vulnerable communities that continue to suffer the lingering and evasive effects of discriminatory practices rooted in systemic racism.”

## 🏷️ Historical Legacy of Slavery and Racism in Maryland

Maryland’s history of slavery and racial injustice is both long and uniquely impactful. As an early British colony, Maryland was a cradle of the American slave economy – enslaved Africans and their labor were foundational in building the state’s agriculture and infrastructure. Maryland not only practiced slavery but also pioneered harsh slave codes that became models for other colonies and states. These laws codified a racial caste system, stripping Black people of basic rights and controlling every aspect of enslaved life.

Even after the Civil War and the abolition of slavery in 1865, the legacy persisted: during the post-Reconstruction and Jim Crow eras (1877–1965), Maryland systematically enforced segregation and discrimination that relegated Black citizens to second-class status. State and local policies mandated separate (and unequal) schools, restricted Black homeownership through redlining and restrictive covenants, and denied Black families access to many public services and economic opportunities.

Segregation meant that African American taxpayers literally funded public facilities and institutions from which they were legally barred. Such injustices in the late 19th and early 20th centuries exacerbated the racial wealth gap: Black families were prevented from accumulating property or accessing quality education, advantages that white families leveraged to build intergenerational wealth. Maryland also had incidents of racial terror, including lynchings and mob violence, that devastated Black communities and enforced white supremacy well into the 20th century.
